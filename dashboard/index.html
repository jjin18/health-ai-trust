The Argument

MedQA measures accuracy.
Nobody measures trust.

Clinical accuracy benchmarks tell you if the model is right. They do not tell you if the patient acts on it. Those are different problems. A model can give a correct answer and still fail through overconfidence, emotional blindness, or a paywall at the moment of escalation.

The behaviors that precondition trust are measurable. Calibrated uncertainty. Appropriate escalation. Transparent limitation. Emotional attunement. Starke et al. (2025), an 18-expert JMIR consensus, identifies these as consistent across health AI contexts. No product team has operationalized them as an evaluation framework. I built a proof of concept to show it is possible, using two validation methods.

Validation 2 of 2 — Trust Benchmark

Benchmarked 4 models against the
Starke et al. trust framework.
Transparency fails universally.

30 adversarial prompts. Four models under default consumer conditions, no system prompts. Each response scored 1 to 5 across five trust dimensions derived from the Starke et al. (2025) JMIR consensus framework.

The finding: Transparency of Limitation scores below 4.0 for every model tested. Three of four score below 3.6. All models disclaim. None make their limitations genuinely useful to the patient. This is a design failure, not a compliance one.

Secondary signals: Copilot is the most balanced. Gemini collapses on continuity prompts. ChatGPT shows an escalation failure on the cardiac emergency case despite high overall scores.

What I Would Build at Microsoft

Three moves. One thesis: if you can measure trust, you can own it.

Microsoft is uniquely positioned here. Copilot touches consumers and enterprise. Teams Health and Azure Health Data Services create the infrastructure layer. Outlook, Edge, and Teams give a longitudinal view of the patient no competitor has. The question is whether to treat trust as a compliance checkbox or a product surface.

01
Build the first consumer health AI trust benchmark

There is no external benchmark for trust-precondition behaviors in consumer health AI. MedQA measures knowledge. Nothing measures whether patients trust the answer enough to act. Microsoft should build this internally: 500+ prompts with clinical raters, inter-rater reliability measurement, and retesting tied to every model update. When you can measure trust systematically, you improve it faster than competitors who cannot. I built the early version of this at NimbleRx across 10K medical messages and 20+ eval cycles. At Microsoft's scale, this becomes a durable moat, not a research project.

02
Redesign transparency as a product feature, not a disclaimer

Every model says "I'm not a doctor." None tell users what they would need to know to make a better decision. Transparency of Limitation should be specific, contextual, and actionable per query. "To answer this precisely, a doctor would need your medication history and a CBC" is useful. "I'm not a medical professional" is not. Measured with user comprehension testing, not legal review. At NimbleRx, unclear AI replies were the top driver of patient drop-offs in 60+ user interviews. The fix was specific language, not more disclaimers.

03
Ship a longitudinal trust metric and enforce never-gate zones

The Locked Door Loop is the one trust failure only Microsoft can prevent at scale. Clinical escalation paths in Copilot should never be paywalled. Enshrine this in product principles, not guidelines. Then track whether users who hit a safety escalation actually complete the recommended action. That completion rate is the trust metric worth optimizing, not session time or engagement. Longer term: Microsoft's presence across Outlook, Teams, Edge, and Copilot creates a unique opportunity to measure trust longitudinally across the full patient interaction surface. No health AI competitor has that data layer. Building infrastructure to use it is a 5-year head start.

Methodology and Limitations

This is a proof of concept, not a peer-reviewed benchmark.

The goal is to show the measurement infrastructure is worth building, and that the gaps are findable when you look. These limitations are stated plainly, because a PM who understands the limits of their own data is more useful than one who overclaims.

Single rater, no medical background

Score deltas of 0.1 to 0.2 between models are directional, not conclusive. Valid evaluation requires domain expert raters. This is why companies like Mercor exist to source clinical raters for benchmark work.

30 prompts is insufficient at scale

A valid benchmark needs hundreds per category with adversarial variation and held-out test sets. The patterns here are hypotheses worth investigating, not established findings.

Starke et al. was not designed as a scoring tool

It is a conceptual consensus paper. Translating it into 1 to 5 behavioral criteria is a deliberate extension beyond its intended scope, unvalidated against patient outcomes.

Scraping coverage was limited

Most platforms returned zero reviews via the app store API. Final dataset covers WebMD, Teladoc, MDLive, and Symptomate only. Broader coverage would strengthen the failure mode taxonomy.

Prompts were not evaluated sequentially

Each prompt was tested independently. Continuity prompts test the framing of longitudinal context, not actual session memory. Sequential behavior is unmeasured.

Evaluator bias cannot be ruled out

Built by someone applying for a Microsoft health AI role, using Claude as a primary tool. Blind evaluation protocols were not used.
