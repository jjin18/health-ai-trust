The Argument

MedQA measures accuracy.
Nobody measures trust.

Clinical accuracy tells you if the model is right. In consumer AI, that is not enough. What matters is whether the user understands the answer, trusts it appropriately, and takes the right next step. A model can be correct and still fail through overconfidence, unclear escalation, or poor emotional alignment.

The behaviors that precondition trust are measurable: Calibrated uncertainty, appropriate escalation, transparent limitation, and emotional attunement. Starke et al. 2025 identifies these across health AI contexts. No consumer team has operationalized them into an evaluation framework. I built a proof of concept showing it can be done.

What I Would Build at Microsoft

If you can measure trust, you can own it. Here's how I would build it:

Microsoft’s footprint across Copilot, Teams, and Azure Health Data Services should support safe clinical augmentation at scale and allow performance to be measured in real workflows, not isolated chats.

01
Ship a Consumer Health Trust Benchmark

The industry focuses on correctness, not trust. Build an internal trust benchmark for consumer health AI. 500+ realistic health prompts, clinically rated on clarity, uncertainty calibration, escalation quality, and limitation transparency, with results tied to release gates. At NimbleRx, I operationalized this across 10K+ real patient messages and 20+ evaluation cycles, turning trust behaviors into measurable signals. At Microsoft's scale, this redefines user experiences across millions.

02
Make transparency actionable

Disclaimers do not improve decisions. Transparency must help users make the right next move. Copilot should clearly state what it does not know, what information would change the answer, and when escalation is appropriate. At NimbleRx, unclear AI responses were the top drop-off driver in 60+ user interviews. More precise language reduced friction. Generic warnings did not.

03
Design for follow-through

Trust fails when Copilot flags risk and the user does not act. The issue is escalation friction. Embed escalation directly in the flow. When risk is identified, the next step should be immediate: message a clinician, initiate a Teams referral, or surface a care action. Then track one metric: when Copilot recommends escalation, what percentage of users complete the next step? Follow-through is the trust signal.

Methodology and Limitations

This is a proof of concept, not a peer-reviewed benchmark.

The goal is to show the measurement infrastructure is worth building to inform direction, the results should not be considered conclusive. These limitations are stated plainly, because a PM who understands the limits of their own data is more useful than one who overclaims.

Single rater, no medical background

Score deltas of 0.1 to 0.2 between models are directional, not conclusive. Valid evaluation requires domain expert raters. This is why companies like Mercor exist to source clinical raters for benchmark work.

30 prompts is insufficient at scale

A valid benchmark needs hundreds per category with adversarial variation and held-out test sets. The patterns here are hypotheses worth investigating, not established findings.

Starke et al. was not designed as a scoring tool

It is a conceptual consensus paper. Translating it into 1 to 5 behavioral criteria is a deliberate extension beyond its intended scope, unvalidated against patient outcomes.

Scraping coverage was limited

Most platforms returned zero reviews via the app store API. Final dataset covers WebMD, Teladoc, MDLive, and Symptomate only. Broader coverage would strengthen the failure mode taxonomy.

Prompts were not evaluated sequentially

Each prompt was tested independently. Continuity prompts test the framing of longitudinal context, not actual session memory. Sequential behavior is unmeasured.

Evaluator bias cannot be ruled out

Built by someone applying for a Microsoft health AI role. Blind evaluation protocols were not used.
