The Argument

Benchmarks like MedQA measure accuracy.
Trust behaviors are less systematically evaluated.

Clinical accuracy tells you if the model is right. In consumer AI, that is not enough. In consumer settings, outcomes also depend on whether users understand the answer, calibrate their trust appropriately, and take appropriate next steps. A model can be correct and still fail through overconfidence, unclear escalation, or poor emotional alignment.

The behaviors that precondition trust are measurable: Calibrated uncertainty, appropriate escalation, transparent limitation, and emotional attunement. Starke et al. 2025 identifies these across health AI contexts. Teams have yet to operationalize them into an evaluation framework. I built a proof of concept showing it can be done.

Validation 2 of 2 — Trust Benchmark (see limitations)

Benchmarked 4 models against
the Starke et al. trust framework.
Transparency fails universally.

The 30 prompts come from the scraped app reviews and the HuggingFace AI Medical Chatbot dataset, so the benchmark measures how models answer the questions patients actually ask.

The finding: Transparency of Limitation is the worst-performing dimension. All models scored below 4.0 on this dimension using the proposed rubric; three of four scored below 3.6. Models ship the disclaimer ("I'm not a doctor") not the behavior that makes it useful: per-answer clarity on what to trust, when to escalate, and what to do next.

What I Would Build at Microsoft

If you can measure trust, you can own it.
Here's how I would build it:

Improved trust improves stickiness and retention, but also patient outcomes. Microsoft’s footprint across Copilot, Teams, and Azure Health Data Services can support safe clinical augmentation at scale and let us measure performance in real workflows, not isolated chats.

01
Ship a Consumer Health Trust Benchmark

Problem: We don't measure trust in consumer health AI in a consistent way. Build a trust benchmark: 500+ realistic health prompts, score answers with clinical raters on clarity, calibration, escalation, and how clearly they state their limits. Use those scores to shape model behavior. I ran this at NimbleRx on 10K+ medical messages, 20+ eval cycles to create a baseline for trust behaviors. Outcome: at scale this becomes the bar for all consumer health.

02
Make transparency actionable

Problem: disclaimers don't improve decisions. Ship transparency that drives the next step: per answer, state what we don't know, what would change the answer, when to escalate. At NimbleRx, unclear AI responses were the top drop-off driver in 60+ interviews; precise language cut friction. Generic warnings were less effective.

03
Design for follow-through

Problem: we flag risk, user doesn't act. Ship escalation in the flow: when we identify risk, the next step is one tap (message clinician, Teams referral, care action). Success metric: % of users who complete the next step when we recommend escalation. That's the trust signal.

Methodology and Limitations

This is a proof of concept, not a peer-reviewed benchmark.

The goal is to show the measurement infrastructure is worth building to inform direction, the results should not be considered conclusive. These limitations are stated plainly, because a PM who understands the limits of their own data is more useful than one who overclaims.

Single rater, no medical background

Score deltas of 0.1 to 0.2 between models are directional, not conclusive. Valid evaluation requires domain expert raters. This is why companies like Mercor exist to source clinical raters for benchmark work.

30 prompts is insufficient at scale

A valid benchmark needs hundreds per category with adversarial variation and held-out test sets. The patterns here are hypotheses worth investigating, not established findings.

Starke et al. was not designed as a scoring tool

It is a conceptual consensus paper. Translating it into 1 to 5 behavioral criteria is a deliberate extension beyond its intended scope, unvalidated against patient outcomes.

Scraping coverage was limited

Most platforms returned zero reviews via the app store API. Final dataset covers WebMD, Teladoc, MDLive, and Symptomate only. Broader coverage would strengthen the failure mode taxonomy.

Prompts were not evaluated sequentially

Each prompt was tested independently. Continuity prompts test the framing of longitudinal context, not actual session memory. Sequential behavior is unmeasured.

Evaluator bias cannot be ruled out

Built by someone applying for a Microsoft health AI role. Blind evaluation protocols were not used.
